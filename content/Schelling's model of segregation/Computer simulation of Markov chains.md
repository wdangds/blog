## 1. Computer Simulation of Markov Chains
"A key matter in many (most?) practical applications of Markov theory is the ability to simulate Markov chains on a computer." Computer simulation is a **key matter in many practical applications of Markov theory**. A core assumption, although technically *incorrect*, is that we have access to a sequence of **[[Basics of probability theory#2. Random Variables and Distributions|i.i.d random variables]], uniformly distributed on the unit interval** (denoted as $U_0, U_1,....$).

This initial assumption about random number generators being *truly* random and uniformly distributed is a simplification. There are two main reasons why this is a *lie*:
### a. Non-Uniform Distribution
- The number obtained from computer random number generators (RNGs), **are not uniformly distributed on $[0,1]$**.
- Typically, these numbers have a **finite binary (or decimal) expansion**, meaning that they are *rational*.
- In contrast, a truly uniformly distributed random variable on $[0,1]$ (or any continuous random variable) is **irrational with probability 1** [^1].
- However, this issue is often not a significant problem if the number of binary or decimal digits is reasonably large (e.g., 32 bits).

[^1]: For $U\sim \text{Unif}[0,1]$: for any specific rational $q$, $P(U=q)=0$. Rationals in $[0,1]$ are countable, so $P(U\text{ is rational})=\sum_{q\in \mathbb{Q}\cap[0,1]}P(U=q)=0$. Here $P(U \text{ is irrational})=1$: almost every draw is irrational. 
### b. Deterministic Nature (Pseudo-Randomness)
- **The number $U_0, U_1, ...$ are not truly random**; they are generated by a **deterministic procedure**.
- For this reason, they are most accurately called **pseudo-random number generators**.
- While physically generated sequences of random-looking numbers exists (e.g., https://www.random.org/sequences/), we should have **a healthy dose of skepticism towards claims of their "true" randomness**.
The **deterministic nature (B) is the most important objection** compared to the non-uniform distribution (A). Considerable effort has been made to construct pseudo-random number generators whose output is **as indistinguishable as possible from a true i.i.d sequence of uniform random variables**. Today, **generators exists that perform very well**, passing standard statistical tests. 

Despite these advancements, it is crucial to remember that **pseudo-randomness is a potential source of errors in computer simulation**. A long period (time until repetition) for a generator is **far from sufficient** to deem it good, as certain patterns may occur too frequently.
